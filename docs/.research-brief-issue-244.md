# Research brief — issue #244 Brand Identity & Logo for LinkedIn Buddy

## Executive summary

Issue #244 asks for a complete brand identity that feels cute, memorable, and
developer-native without drifting toward LinkedIn's corporate visual language.

The strongest opportunity is not to look more like LinkedIn. It is to look
more human, more open-source, and more tool-centric:

- current high-star developer tools win with a bold silhouette, one dark anchor
  color, one warm accent, and a mark that can shrink to a favicon
- LinkedIn-adjacent competitors skew literal and functional, with heavy
  "scraper", "automation", or growth-hacking language and very little warmth
- GitHub social previews reward one clear focal mark, short copy, and enough
  contrast to survive Slack, Discord, and Twitter link cards

That led to the recommended direction shipped in this branch:

- mascot direction: `Beacon Buddy`
- visual metaphor: a friendly speech-bubble helper with a signal spark
- palette: deep ink + mint + coral + warm cream
- differentiation strategy: approachable mascot and non-blue palette rather
  than corporate-network visuals

## Research questions

### 1) What do highly starred developer-tool repos do visually?

On 2026-03-11, I sampled 50 current high-star repositories from GitHub search
across CLI, API, MCP, and general developer-tool buckets. The goal was not to
produce a mathematically perfect category ranking; it was to capture the visual
patterns contributors actually see when starring tools on GitHub.

Representative repos in the sample:

- `ohmyzsh/ohmyzsh`
- `google-gemini/gemini-cli`
- `junegunn/fzf`
- `jesseduffield/lazygit`
- `cli/cli`
- `httpie/cli`
- `hoppscotch/hoppscotch`
- `firecrawl/firecrawl`

Repeated patterns:

- the icon or mascot is simpler than the product itself
- a strong silhouette matters more than internal detail
- one dark neutral plus one vivid accent reads as modern and trustworthy
- friendly motion or anthropomorphic cues make tools feel more approachable
- the best marks still work when reduced to a monochrome glyph

### 2) What makes a logo memorable and "starrable"?

Cross-checking brand-design guidance against the GitHub scan produced the same
few rules over and over:

- keep the shape simple enough to recognize in under a second
- favor one distinct outline over multiple small details
- make sure the mark survives at favicon scale before polishing the hero asset
- use limited color roles so the brand feels consistent in badges, README art,
  and social cards
- give the mark a little personality so it feels like a project people want to
  root for, not just a utility they happen to use

### 3) How are LinkedIn-adjacent competitors positioned?

Current LinkedIn-adjacent open-source repos and packages are concentrated in a
few clusters:

- scraping libraries such as `joeyism/linkedin_scraper`
- outreach or lead-gen tools such as `eracle/OpenOutreach`
- job-application automation such as `GodsScion/Auto_job_applier_linkedIn`
- newer assistant integrations such as `stickerdaniel/linkedin-mcp-server`

Visual and naming patterns in that space are mostly:

- literal LinkedIn blue
- generic dashboard or bot imagery
- value props centered on scraping, scale, or growth
- very little warmth, charm, or mascot-led branding

That creates a clear differentiation lane for LinkedIn Buddy:

- friendly instead of extractive
- assistant-like instead of bot-like
- developer-tooling cues instead of sales-automation cues
- mint/coral/ink instead of LinkedIn-adjacent blue

### 4) What should the social preview optimize for?

GitHub's guidance still centers on a `1280x640` social preview image. In
practice that means:

- a single dominant focal area
- short copy that stays readable on mobile previews
- generous outer padding so Slack and Twitter crops do not clip the title
- enough tonal contrast that the image still works after platform compression

## Design recommendation

### Chosen direction: Beacon Buddy

The shipped identity uses a rounded speech-bubble mascot with a small signal
spark. That combination does a few jobs at once:

- speech bubble: messaging, connection, and conversation
- rounded geometry: friendly and cute without becoming childish
- spark: a light technical cue that suggests activity, awareness, and polish
- simple face: immediate memorability and easy favicon scaling

### Why this works for the product

- It matches the "Buddy" name directly.
- It stays distinct from LinkedIn's square `in` logo and corporate palette.
- It reads as a tool for people, not a scraper for harvesting data.
- It can flex from `16px` browser chrome to a large README/social hero.
